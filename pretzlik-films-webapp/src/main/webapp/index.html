Biography

Luke Pretzlik

Luke Pretzlik was born in October 1973 in London. He got straight A's at his exams and went on to study Zoology at Bristol University. With a bursary from the British Army he bought a cheap Hi8 video camera and made a road movie through the Amazon and the Pantanal region of Brazil on graduating.

He took the film to the BBC Natural History Unit and on the back of that and a good interview got a job and started work immediately on a series on Central American wildlife called Spirit of the Jaguar. From there he moved on to Green Umbrella to work on a series about European wildlife for various European networks.

Other credits include the twice BAFTA nominated film Operation Lemur featuring John Cleese in Madagascar (Tiger Aspect) and Congo, a 3 part series about Central African wildlife (Scorer Associates), both broadcast by the BBC.

He worked on blue chip programming for some 6 years and then went independent. Working independently he could pick and choose the projects he worked on more easily and developed his skills as a better all round film making which would include more writing, directing and editing.

More recently he has produced documentaries about mental health some of which were commissioned by Mind, the leading mental health charity. Because of his own struggle with manic depression he has huge insight into it. A self confessed expert.

Currently he is working closely with Blink Films UK on a number of projects.

Online Film School

Introduction to film making.

So you’re just starting out in film making. Here’s a simple guide that will get you on the right track.

Film making is an art form like any other and so needs talent and dedication to be really good at it. In professional film making it is seldom a solo effort but often involves a crew of people. Documentaries tend to need less equipment and less manpower than a drama and so can be done on a smaller budget.

You can make a film from scratch for under £400. It is potentially quite an expensive pass time with Hollywood budgets running into the 100’s of million but these days consumer equipment is of such good quality that it can be done for very little if need be. You can pick up a High Definition camera for under £400 and some free editing software on the net and that’s the minimum that you need.

If your story is good then the equipment that you use can suffer. When the twin towers fell did anyone complain that it wasn’t in HD? Some stories are so exceptional that you can film them on your phone camera, in fact if you don’t want to invest in any equipment then use your phone camera. So what if you don’t look the part, at least you’ll no how to operate it and can be more stealth like.

There are a number of stages in film making and the most important stage is coming up with a good idea. Give plenty of time for your idea to mature but be realistic about whether it is realisable or not given your skills, contacts, budget and luck.

There are 5 stages to film making:

  • Development
  • Pre-production
  • Production
  • Post-production
  • Distribution
